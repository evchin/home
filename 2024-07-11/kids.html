preface

i grew up mostly raised by my mom. in an immigrant family from taiwan, there was a lot of tough love. it was only recently that i was able to acknowledge that without anger. while i won't be raising my kids the same way i was brought up, i get now that intentions do matter. i'm grateful to my mother for doing her best. i think she did a great job with the hand she was played.

i think being a kid is really really hard. i think it can be the hardest thing some people have to go through. if you're born with a bad hand, there's not really any way out. you don't get choices, and you don't get control. your boundaries get to be broken everyday and you get forced to do things you don't want to do and everyone laughs when you say you're having a hard time because how could you? you're just a kid.

growing up, i was told everyday that life gets harder the older you get. but i swear to you and me and the whole world that those words are so completely and utterly dead wrong that they should have a gravestone of their own. i hope there's a kid out there that's got the spunk in them to make it to 18 so they can make a life of their own and build the family they never had. if you make it to 18, everything else is relatively easy, and i have never believed in anything else so hard in my life.

morning will come, it has no choice. (marty rubin, canadian gay activist)

things i've thought about so far

  • no asking them "what do you wanna be". just "what do you wanna do".

  • no public school. i don't think the school i want for my kid exists yet. i'd like to either build it, or homeschool them.

  • exposure to so many different people and places that they have no concept of what a "normal" person should be.

  • reading books (at least when they're young). knowledge of what good writing is must be built into their bones.

  • letting them make their mistakes (as long as they won't be permanently injured or die)

  • no hitting. it's stupid, primal, lazy, and sadistic. it's immensely disconcerting to be hurt by someone one second and be hugged by them another.

  • no yelling. it's also primal and lazy.

  • learning a musical instrument. fluency in another medium of expression is so gratifying.

  • grow up multilingual. i intend to become fluent enough in mandarin to the point where i can talk to my kid in it and not feel totally awkward. i'd also like to learn a language together.

  • shopping in all sections of the store. none of that boy/girl shit.

  • eat dinner together. no phones.

thoughts on corporal punishment

there's something about being hit as a kid that just feels like it'll always be the worst thing that you'll ever have to deal with for your whole life.

the only time i ever cried during therapy was when my therapist was legally required to ask me three questions. what i said in response would determine if she would have to call CPS on my mom. and i remember just starting to cry in a way that i hadn't cried in since i was a little kid. i couldn't stop the tears, and i wasn't even close.

thoughts on a chinese character: 乖

there is a word in chinese that i had to hear an insufferable number of times growing up: 乖 (romanization: "guai"). it means "well-behaved", most often in reference to a child by an elder person.

it always felt like the most condescending, patronizing, and humiliating way to be referred to. "yes! good job for doing exactly what i tell you to do!"

i remember very viscerally that it always gave me the urge to hit the person who was saying it. i wanted to hurt them. i wanted to be violent. i was probably 6-10yo.

calling a kid 乖 feels like taking all of the powerfulness they feel, compressing it into a ball, and then pitching it straight into their gut with all of your adult might. it's incredibly cruel. and i can only imagine how dangerous it'd be if you raised a kid to see it like a compliment the way the adults always phrase it to be.
